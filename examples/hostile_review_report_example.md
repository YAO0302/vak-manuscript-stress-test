# Hostile Review Report Example

This is an example of a Hostile Peer Review Report.

It shows what the output may look like after using:

```text
prompts/06_hostile_peer_reviewer.md
```

This example is fictional. It does not review a real manuscript.

## Manuscript topic

Transformation of elements of traditional Chinese culture in contemporary architecture and their relation to active longevity.

## Target field

Cultural studies, architecture theory, humanities.

## 1. Simulated editorial decision

Decision:

Major revision.

The manuscript has a potentially publishable topic, but it is not ready for submission in its current form. The main problem is not language quality. The main problem is the weak alignment between research question, method, evidence, and conclusion.

The article attempts to connect traditional Chinese cultural elements, contemporary architecture, and active longevity, but the mechanism linking these elements remains insufficiently explained. The manuscript risks making claims that are broader than its evidence allows.

## 2. Main rejection risks

### Fatal risks

1. The manuscript does not clearly distinguish between architectural-cultural analysis and health-related claims.
2. The method cannot support direct claims about active longevity unless empirical evidence is provided.
3. The research question is too broad and contains several different studies inside one article.
4. The conclusion risks exceeding the material.

### Fixable risks

1. The introduction can be rebuilt around a clearer research gap.
2. The methodology can be rewritten to explain how semiotic, comparative, and historical-cultural analysis work together.
3. The main body can be reorganized around a smaller number of analytical claims.
4. Claims about active longevity can be reframed through environmental parameters, everyday practices, social interaction, and mobility.

## 3. Research question assessment

The manuscript appears to ask how elements of traditional Chinese culture are transformed in contemporary architecture and how this transformation relates to active longevity.

This is too broad in its current form.

The question combines at least three different problems:

1. transformation of traditional cultural elements;
2. architectural representation and spatial organization;
3. active longevity and human practices.

A stronger research question would be:

```text
How are elements of traditional Chinese culture transformed in contemporary architectural environments associated with practices of active longevity?
```

This version is narrower and more suitable for cultural and architectural analysis.

## 4. Method-question mismatch

The manuscript names semiotic analysis, comparative analysis, and historical-cultural analysis, but it does not show how these methods produce the article's findings.

The current method can support claims about:

* symbolic transformation;
* cultural memory;
* visual representation;
* architectural form;
* spatial organization.

The current method cannot by itself support direct claims about:

* biological aging;
* measurable health improvement;
* life expectancy;
* medical outcomes;
* direct causal influence of architecture on longevity.

If the author wants to make direct claims about active longevity, the manuscript needs empirical evidence from gerontology, environmental psychology, public health, or social research.

If the author does not have such evidence, the claims must be narrowed.

## 5. Object-subject-aim-task consistency

### Object

The object appears to be contemporary architectural environment in China.

This is acceptable.

### Subject

The subject appears to be the transformation of traditional Chinese cultural elements in contemporary architecture in relation to active longevity.

This is too broad unless the author defines the exact parameters of active longevity.

### Aim

The aim should not promise to prove the influence of traditional culture on active longevity.

A stronger aim would be:

```text
The aim of the article is to identify the ways in which elements of traditional Chinese culture are transformed in contemporary architectural environments and to determine how these elements participate in shaping spatial conditions associated with active longevity practices.
```

### Tasks

The tasks should be rebuilt as follows:

1. Define traditional cultural elements in relation to architectural space.
2. Explain the methodological basis for analyzing these elements.
3. Identify selected architectural cases or materials.
4. Analyze how traditional elements are transformed in contemporary architectural form.
5. Interpret their relation to social interaction, mobility, orientation, and everyday practices.

## 6. Logical breaks and unsupported transitions

### Break 1

The manuscript moves from cultural tradition to active longevity too quickly.

Problem:

The connection between symbolic architectural elements and active longevity is asserted rather than demonstrated.

Required fix:

Introduce an intermediate mechanism:

```text
architectural environment → spatial practices → social interaction / mobility / orientation → active longevity practices
```

### Break 2

The manuscript describes traditional cultural elements but does not explain how they are transformed.

Problem:

Description replaces analysis.

Required fix:

For each element, show:

1. traditional meaning or function;
2. contemporary architectural form;
3. transformation of function;
4. relation to spatial experience or practice.

### Break 3

The methodology appears after broad theoretical claims.

Problem:

The reader does not understand how the claims will be tested or interpreted.

Required fix:

Move the methodology earlier and connect it directly to the research question.

## 7. Identification gaps

The manuscript does not clearly explain how it identifies a "traditional cultural element."

The author must define criteria such as:

* visual reference;
* symbolic form;
* spatial principle;
* material or decorative system;
* relation to cultural memory;
* continuity with historical architectural forms.

The manuscript also does not explain how it identifies a link to active longevity.

Possible criteria include:

* support for walking or bodily mobility;
* support for social interaction;
* orientation and readability of space;
* access to public or semi-public areas;
* relation to everyday practices;
* environmental comfort;
* symbolic familiarity and cultural identity.

Without such criteria, the argument remains impressionistic.

## 8. Theoretical framework problems

The theoretical framework appears decorative.

The manuscript names cultural memory, tradition, architectural environment, and active longevity, but these concepts are not fully integrated.

Required fix:

Define only the concepts needed for analysis.

Recommended conceptual chain:

```text
traditional cultural element
↓
architectural transformation
↓
spatial environment
↓
everyday practice
↓
active longevity
```

Do not introduce additional concepts unless they are used in the analysis.

## 9. Literature review weaknesses

The literature review appears to list sources instead of synthesizing them.

It should be reorganized into groups:

1. studies on traditional Chinese culture and cultural memory;
2. studies on contemporary Chinese architecture;
3. studies on architectural environment and everyday practices;
4. studies on active longevity and environmental factors.

The gap should appear after these groups.

Possible gap:

```text
Existing research often discusses traditional cultural elements as symbols or heritage forms, while their transformation in contemporary architectural environments associated with active longevity practices remains insufficiently clarified.
```

## 10. Evidence and material problems

The manuscript must specify its material.

Currently unclear:

* which architectural objects are analyzed;
* why these objects were selected;
* whether they are representative;
* what documentation supports the analysis;
* whether photographs, plans, descriptions, or field observations are used.

Required fix:

Create a material section with selection criteria.

Possible criteria:

* presence of traditional cultural elements;
* contemporary architectural context;
* relevance to public or everyday use;
* connection to social interaction or mobility;
* availability of visual or textual documentation.

## 11. Novelty assessment

Novelty level:

Moderate but currently overstated.

The topic has potential novelty because it connects cultural tradition, architecture, and active longevity. However, the manuscript must show that this connection is not merely thematic.

The strongest possible novelty is not:

```text
No one has studied this topic before.
```

A better novelty claim is:

```text
The novelty of the article lies in interpreting traditional Chinese cultural elements not only as decorative or symbolic forms, but as components of architectural environment connected to spatial practices associated with active longevity.
```

## 12. Paragraph-level problems

### Introduction

Problem:

Too broad and not sufficiently problem-driven.

Required fix:

Begin with the specific problem of cultural elements in contemporary architectural environments.

### Literature review

Problem:

Sources are listed but not organized into a research gap.

Required fix:

Group literature by approach and end with a clear gap.

### Methodology

Problem:

Methods are listed but not explained.

Required fix:

Explain how each method works.

### Main analysis

Problem:

Several paragraphs describe examples without explaining what they show.

Required fix:

Use the sequence:

```text
claim → material → analysis → interpretation → link to research question
```

### Conclusion

Problem:

Conclusion risks claiming more than the article proves.

Required fix:

Narrow conclusion to spatial, cultural, and practice-related findings.

## 13. Likely reviewer questions

1. What exactly is the research question?
2. How is "traditional Chinese cultural element" defined?
3. Why were these architectural objects selected?
4. How does the method identify transformation?
5. What evidence connects architecture to active longevity?
6. Are health-related claims supported by relevant literature?
7. Does the conclusion exceed the available material?
8. What is the article's real novelty?
9. Is the theoretical framework used in the analysis or only mentioned?
10. Why is this manuscript suitable for the target ВАК journal?

## 14. Author response strategy

Do not respond by adding defensive formulas such as:

```text
This article does not claim that architecture directly causes active longevity.
```

Instead, correct the structure.

Use positive analytical framing:

```text
The article examines active longevity through environmental parameters connected to social interaction, bodily mobility, orientation, and everyday practices.
```

The response strategy should be:

1. narrow the research question;
2. clarify object and subject;
3. define active longevity parameters;
4. explain the method;
5. specify material and selection criteria;
6. narrow unsupported claims;
7. rebuild conclusion around supported findings.

## 15. Structural reconstruction plan

Recommended new structure:

1. Introduction

   * relevance;
   * research gap;
   * object, subject, aim, tasks;
   * method and material.

2. Literature review

   * cultural tradition and architectural symbolism;
   * contemporary architecture and cultural memory;
   * active longevity and environmental parameters.

3. Methodology and material

   * selected cases;
   * selection criteria;
   * semiotic, comparative, and historical-cultural analysis.

4. Transformation of traditional cultural elements

   * visual and symbolic transformation;
   * spatial transformation;
   * functional transformation.

5. Architectural environment and active longevity practices

   * social interaction;
   * mobility;
   * orientation;
   * everyday practices.

6. Conclusion

   * supported findings;
   * contribution;
   * future research.

## 16. Priority rewrite zones

1. Research question, aim, and subject.
2. Methodology section.
3. Literature review and research gap.
4. Main analytical section.
5. Conclusion.
6. Abstract.

The title and keywords should be revised after the main argument is stabilized.

## 17. Final recommendation

Final recommendation:

Rebuild argument before submission.

The manuscript has a promising topic but is not ready for submission until the research question is narrowed, the methodology is connected to the material, and the active longevity claims are reframed through supported environmental and social mechanisms.
