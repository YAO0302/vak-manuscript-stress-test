# Hostile Review Checklist

This checklist is used to stress-test an academic manuscript before submission.

It simulates the questions a strict reviewer or editor may ask when deciding whether the manuscript should be rejected, revised, or accepted.

## Purpose

The goal is to identify weaknesses before submission.

This checklist focuses on:

* research question;
* method-question alignment;
* object, subject, aim, and tasks;
* logical structure;
* evidence;
* novelty;
* literature review;
* theoretical framework;
* journal fit;
* rejection risks.

## Basic rule

Do not ask whether the manuscript sounds academic.

Ask whether it can survive a strict review.

A manuscript can sound academic and still be weak.

A manuscript can have polished language and still fail because of method, logic, evidence, or novelty.

## 1. Research question

Check:

* Is there a clear research question?
* Is the research question explicitly stated?
* Is it answerable within one article?
* Is it narrower than the general topic?
* Is it connected to the introduction?
* Is it actually answered in the conclusion?
* Does the manuscript study a problem rather than merely describe a topic?

High-risk signs:

* the article has a topic but no question;
* the research question is too broad;
* the question appears only indirectly;
* the conclusion does not answer the question;
* the question changes during the article.

## 2. Object, subject, aim, and tasks

Check:

* Is the object of research clear?
* Is the subject of research narrower than the object?
* Does the aim follow from the research question?
* Do the tasks lead logically to the aim?
* Does each task correspond to a section or analytical step?
* Are the tasks actually completed?
* Does the conclusion return to the aim?

High-risk signs:

* object and subject are confused;
* aim is broader than the article can handle;
* tasks are decorative;
* tasks are listed but not used;
* conclusion does not match the stated aim.

## 3. Method-question alignment

Check:

* Does the chosen method answer the research question?
* Is the method connected to the material?
* Is the method explained, not merely named?
* Is the method actually used in the analysis?
* Does the method produce the type of conclusion the author claims?
* Are several methods listed without showing how they work together?

High-risk signs:

* method appears only once in the introduction;
* method is a decorative list;
* method does not match the evidence;
* method is too broad to be meaningful;
* conclusion requires a method that was not used.

## 4. Material and evidence

Check:

* What material is analyzed?
* Why was this material selected?
* Is the selection justified?
* Is the material sufficient?
* Are examples analyzed or only mentioned?
* Does the evidence support the claims?
* Are there claims that require stronger evidence?

High-risk signs:

* case selection is not explained;
* examples are illustrative but not analytical;
* conclusion is broader than the material;
* evidence appears after the claim instead of supporting it;
* the manuscript treats limited cases as general proof.

## 5. Logical structure

Check:

* Does the article move step by step?
* Are transitions between sections clear?
* Does each paragraph have a function?
* Are definitions introduced before they are used?
* Does the method appear at the right moment?
* Does the article avoid unnecessary repetition?
* Does the conclusion follow from the analysis?

High-risk signs:

* sudden jumps between ideas;
* repeated claims without development;
* technical detail appears too early;
* literature review does not lead to the research gap;
* paragraphs combine too many tasks;
* conclusion introduces new unsupported claims.

## 6. Identification gaps

Check how the manuscript knows what it claims to know.

Ask:

* How does the author identify the phenomenon?
* What counts as evidence?
* How are examples selected?
* How does the author move from observation to interpretation?
* Is there a hidden causal claim?
* Is correlation being treated as causation?
* Are descriptive observations presented as analytical findings?

High-risk signs:

* the text asserts transformation but does not show how it is identified;
* the text claims influence but does not explain the mechanism;
* the text uses cultural concepts without operationalizing them;
* the text moves from architectural description to social effect too quickly;
* the text makes health, longevity, or social claims without adequate evidence.

## 7. Theoretical framework

Check:

* Is the theoretical framework necessary?
* Are key concepts defined?
* Are theoretical sources actually used?
* Does theory guide the analysis?
* Are concepts used consistently?
* Does the manuscript avoid decorative theory?

High-risk signs:

* theory is named but not applied;
* concepts are introduced and then disappear;
* theoretical vocabulary replaces analysis;
* concepts from different fields are mixed without explanation;
* theory is used to decorate the introduction.

## 8. Literature review

Check:

* Does the literature review create a research gap?
* Are sources synthesized rather than listed?
* Are key authors included?
* Are recent sources included?
* Are foundational sources included?
* Does the literature review explain what remains unresolved?
* Does it lead naturally to the author's research question?

High-risk signs:

* literature review is a list of names;
* no clear research gap;
* outdated literature dominates;
* missing major sources;
* sources are cited but not discussed;
* literature review does not connect to the article's aim.

## 9. Citation and reference risk

Check:

* Are all references real?
* Are suspicious sources marked for verification?
* Does every in-text citation have a bibliography entry?
* Is every bibliography entry cited?
* Do cited sources support the claims?
* Are DOI, year, volume, issue, and pages correct?
* Are there signs of AI-generated references?

High-risk signs:

* plausible but unverifiable references;
* DOI mismatch;
* real journal with fake article;
* real author with fake title;
* citations used as decoration;
* unsupported claims with citations attached.

## 10. Novelty and contribution

Check:

* What is new in the manuscript?
* Is the novelty theoretical, methodological, material, interpretive, or practical?
* Is the novelty stated clearly?
* Is the novelty demonstrated, not merely announced?
* Does the conclusion show what the manuscript contributes?
* Is the contribution appropriate for the target journal?

High-risk signs:

* novelty is only in the topic;
* novelty is overstated;
* contribution is unclear;
* the article repeats known claims;
* conclusion does not identify a real contribution.

## 11. Target journal fit

Check:

* Does the manuscript match the target journal's field?
* Does it match the journal's article structure?
* Does it use the expected academic style?
* Does it meet the expected level of method explanation?
* Does it cite literature relevant to the journal's discipline?
* Does it match the journal's scope and readership?

High-risk signs:

* article belongs to another field;
* too little methodological explanation;
* too much general background;
* style is too essayistic;
* bibliography is outside the journal's discipline;
* contribution is not relevant to the journal's audience.

## 12. Defensive writing risk

Check:

* Does the manuscript overuse defensive formulas?
* Does it repeatedly say what it does not claim?
* Does it answer objections before developing the argument?
* Does it add unnecessary explanations to avoid criticism?
* Does the writing burden the first-time reader?

High-risk signs:

* repeated phrases such as "we do not claim that";
* repeated phrases such as "this does not mean that";
* repeated phrases such as "the article does not attempt to";
* Russian equivalents such as "мы не утверждаем, что";
* Russian equivalents such as "это не означает, что";
* Russian equivalents such as "настоящая статья не ставит целью";
* paragraphs sound like replies to reviewers rather than article prose.

## 14. Rejection risk classification

Classify the manuscript.

### Low risk

The manuscript has a clear question, suitable method, sufficient evidence, real references, and coherent structure.

### Moderate risk

The manuscript is publishable after revision but has weaknesses in structure, method explanation, or evidence.

### High risk

The manuscript has serious problems with research question, method, evidence, novelty, or references.

### Critical risk

The manuscript is not ready for submission because it contains fatal flaws, unverifiable references, unsupported claims, or no clear contribution.

## 15. Likely reviewer questions

Before submission, write possible reviewer questions.

Examples:

* What exactly is the research question?
* Why was this material selected?
* How does the method answer the question?
* What is the difference between object and subject?
* What is the actual novelty?
* Which sources support the central claim?
* Why are these examples sufficient?
* How does the conclusion follow from the analysis?
* What makes this article suitable for this journal?
* Are the references real and verifiable?

## 16. Required revision actions

After the review, classify actions into three groups.

### Must fix before submission

Problems that can lead to rejection.

### Should fix before submission

Problems that weaken the manuscript but may not be fatal.

### Optional improvement

Changes that can improve clarity, style, or presentation.

## 17. Final decision

Choose one:

* Ready for submission after minor edits.
* Needs targeted revision.
* Needs major structural revision.
* Needs method and evidence reconstruction.
* Not ready for submission.

## Final rule

The purpose of hostile review is not to make the manuscript cautious.

The purpose is to make it stronger.

Do not hide weak logic behind defensive language.

Fix the logic.
