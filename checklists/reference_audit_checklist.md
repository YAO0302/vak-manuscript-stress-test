# Reference Audit Checklist

This checklist is used to verify whether references in a manuscript are real, consistent, relevant, and suitable for academic publication.

It should be used before submission to a Russian ВАК journal or any other scholarly journal.

## Purpose

The goal of this checklist is to reduce the risk of:

* fabricated references;
* incorrect DOI numbers;
* fake or unverifiable sources;
* mismatch between in-text citations and bibliography;
* unsupported academic claims;
* weak or decorative citations;
* bibliography entries that are not actually used in the manuscript.

## Basic rule

Never submit a manuscript with references that have not been checked.

A reference that looks plausible is not necessarily real.

A DOI that looks correct is not necessarily valid.

A source that exists is not necessarily relevant to the claim it is used to support.

## 1. Bibliographic reality check

For each reference, check:

* Does the source actually exist?
* Are the author names correct?
* Is the title correct?
* Is the journal, book, publisher, or conference real?
* Is the publication year correct?
* Are volume, issue, and page numbers correct?
* Does the DOI exist?
* Does the DOI lead to the same article?
* Is the URL still available?
* Is the language of the source correctly indicated?

Mark each source as:

* verified;
* likely real;
* unverifiable;
* suspicious;
* likely fabricated.

## 2. DOI check

For every DOI, check:

* Does the DOI resolve correctly?
* Does it lead to the same title?
* Does it lead to the same authors?
* Does it lead to the same journal or publisher?
* Does the year match?
* Does the DOI contain obvious formatting errors?

High-risk DOI signs:

* DOI does not resolve;
* DOI leads to a different article;
* DOI belongs to another author;
* DOI belongs to another journal;
* DOI format looks plausible but cannot be found;
* DOI is missing for a source that normally should have one.

## 3. In-text citation and bibliography matching

Check all in-text citations.

For each citation in the manuscript:

* Is there a matching entry in the bibliography?
* Are author names spelled consistently?
* Is the year the same?
* If there are several works by the same author in the same year, are they marked correctly, such as 2020a and 2020b?
* Is transliteration consistent?
* Are Russian and English versions of names handled consistently?

Check all bibliography entries.

For each bibliography entry:

* Is it cited in the manuscript?
* If not, should it be removed?
* Is it relevant to the article?
* Is it included only for decoration?

## 4. Citation-claim alignment

For each important claim, check whether the cited source actually supports it.

Ask:

* Does the source directly support this claim?
* Does it support only part of the claim?
* Is the claim broader than the source allows?
* Is a theoretical source being used as empirical evidence?
* Is a case study being used as proof of a general rule?
* Is the source outdated for this claim?
* Is the source from the correct field?
* Does the paragraph need more than one source?

Mark each claim as:

* supported;
* partially supported;
* weakly supported;
* unsupported;
* requires stronger evidence;
* citation does not match claim.

## 5. Fabrication risk check

Look for signs of AI-generated or fabricated references.

Warning signs:

* real journal, fake article;
* real author, fake title;
* real title, wrong author;
* real article, wrong year;
* real article, wrong volume or pages;
* fake-looking DOI;
* suspiciously generic title;
* title sounds academic but cannot be found;
* journal name translated incorrectly;
* source appears in no database;
* repeated artificial citation patterns;
* bibliography contains too many sources that cannot be traced.

If a source shows several warning signs, do not use it before manual verification.

## 6. Russian academic database check

For Russian-language sources, check where possible:

* eLIBRARY;
* CyberLeninka;
* РИНЦ;
* journal website;
* university repository;
* publisher website;
* library catalog.

For international sources, check where possible:

* Crossref;
* Google Scholar;
* Scopus;
* Web of Science;
* publisher website;
* journal website;
* DOI resolver.

## 7. ВАК-level suitability check

For a Russian ВАК journal manuscript, check:

* Are there enough scholarly sources?
* Are there enough recent sources?
* Are foundational sources included?
* Are Russian-language sources included when necessary?
* Are international sources included when necessary?
* Are non-academic websites overused?
* Are textbooks used where journal articles would be better?
* Are primary sources needed?
* Are key authors in the field missing?
* Is the bibliography too narrow?
* Is the bibliography too decorative?

## 8. Source type balance

Check whether the bibliography includes appropriate source types.

Possible source types:

* peer-reviewed journal articles;
* monographs;
* edited volumes;
* conference papers;
* official documents;
* archival materials;
* primary sources;
* statistical data;
* architectural project documentation;
* museum or exhibition materials;
* dissertations;
* online materials.

High-risk imbalance:

* too many websites;
* too many old sources;
* too many general textbooks;
* too few peer-reviewed articles;
* too few sources in the target discipline;
* too many sources unrelated to the research question.

## 9. Claim risk classification

Classify manuscript claims by evidence risk.

### Low-risk claim

The claim is descriptive and directly supported by a cited source or material.

### Medium-risk claim

The claim is interpretive and needs clear explanation or additional support.

### High-risk claim

The claim is broad, causal, comparative, historical, medical, sociological, or policy-related and requires strong evidence.

### Critical-risk claim

The claim has no source, exceeds the evidence, or relies on a suspicious reference.

Critical-risk claims must be revised before submission.

## 11. Common problems and fixes

### Problem

In-text citation has no bibliography entry.

### Fix

Add the correct bibliography entry only after verifying the source.

Do not invent missing bibliographic details.

### Problem

Bibliography entry is never cited.

### Fix

Remove it unless it is genuinely used in the manuscript.

### Problem

Source exists but does not support the claim.

### Fix

Revise the claim or replace the source with a verified relevant source.

### Problem

DOI leads to another article.

### Fix

Correct the DOI only after checking the official publisher or Crossref record.

### Problem

Reference looks plausible but cannot be found.

### Fix

Mark it as suspicious and do not use it until verified.

### Problem

The source is real but too weak.

### Fix

Find stronger peer-reviewed literature or narrow the claim.

## 12. Final decision

After checking the bibliography, choose one status:

* Ready after minor correction.
* Requires formatting correction.
* Requires substantial verification.
* High risk: do not submit yet.
* Critical risk: bibliography must be rebuilt.

## 13. Final pre-submission rule

Before submission, every reference should satisfy three conditions:

1. The source is real.
2. The citation data are correct.
3. The source supports the claim it is attached to.

If any of these three conditions is not satisfied, the reference is not ready for submission.
