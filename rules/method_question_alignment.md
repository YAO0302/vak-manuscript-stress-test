# Method-Question Alignment

This rule file defines how to check whether the research question, method, material, analysis, and conclusion are aligned.

It is designed for Russian-language academic manuscripts prepared for ВАК journals and related scholarly publications.

## Purpose

A manuscript may sound academic but still fail if the method does not answer the research question.

This file helps identify and correct problems such as:

* unclear research question;
* decorative methodology;
* method list without explanation;
* mismatch between method and material;
* conclusion that exceeds the method;
* broad claims without evidence;
* hidden causal claims;
* weak connection between analysis and result.

## Core principle

The method must be able to produce the type of knowledge claimed in the conclusion.

If the method cannot support the conclusion, the manuscript must either:

1. change the method;
2. narrow the claim;
3. add appropriate evidence;
4. revise the research question;
5. rebuild the argument.

Do not solve method problems by adding defensive formulas.

Fix the logic.

## Alignment chain

A strong manuscript should have a coherent chain:

```text
Research problem
↓
Research question
↓
Object and subject
↓
Aim
↓
Tasks
↓
Material
↓
Method
↓
Analytical procedure
↓
Findings
↓
Conclusion
```

If one part of the chain is missing or inconsistent, the article becomes vulnerable to reviewer criticism.

## 1. Research problem

The research problem should define the unresolved academic issue.

Good research problem:

* specific;
* answerable;
* connected to existing literature;
* not identical to the general topic;
* suitable for one article.

Weak research problem:

* too broad;
* purely descriptive;
* not connected to literature;
* not connected to method;
* cannot be answered with the provided material.

Check question:

```text
What exactly remains unresolved, and why does this article need to address it?
```

## 2. Research question

The research question should guide the whole manuscript.

It should be:

* explicit;
* narrow enough;
* answerable;
* connected to material;
* connected to method;
* answered in the conclusion.

Weak signs:

* the manuscript has a topic but no question;
* the question appears only indirectly;
* the question changes during the article;
* the conclusion answers a different question;
* the method cannot answer the question.

Check question:

```text
Can the selected method and material answer this research question?
```

## 3. Object and subject

The object is the broader phenomenon.

The subject is the specific aspect analyzed in the article.

Rule:

```text
Object must be broader than subject.
Subject must be analyzable through the selected method.
```

Example:

```text
Object: contemporary architectural environment of China.
Subject: transformation of traditional Chinese cultural elements in architectural space in relation to parameters of active longevity.
```

High-risk signs:

* object and subject are identical;
* subject is broader than object;
* subject is not connected to the method;
* subject includes claims the material cannot support;
* subject contains several different studies at once.

## 4. Aim

The aim should follow from the research question.

A good aim names the intellectual operation:

* identify;
* analyze;
* compare;
* classify;
* interpret;
* clarify;
* reveal;
* evaluate, only if criteria are provided.

Weak aims:

* "to study the problem";
* "to consider the topic";
* "to analyze various aspects";
* aim is broader than one article;
* aim promises results the method cannot produce.

Check question:

```text
Can the article realistically achieve this aim with the provided material and method?
```

## 5. Tasks

Tasks should break the aim into concrete steps.

Each task should correspond to a section or analytical step.

Good tasks:

* define key concept;
* review literature;
* justify method;
* analyze selected material;
* compare cases;
* interpret findings;
* formulate conclusion.

Weak tasks:

* decorative;
* too many;
* too broad;
* not completed later;
* not connected to section structure;
* not connected to conclusion.

Check question:

```text
Does each task produce a visible part of the article?
```

## 6. Material

Material is what the author analyzes.

Material may include:

* architectural objects;
* texts;
* documents;
* images;
* spatial plans;
* interviews;
* statistics;
* case studies;
* archival materials;
* scholarly sources;
* policy documents.

The material must be sufficient for the claim.

High-risk signs:

* material is not named;
* selection criteria are absent;
* examples are illustrative only;
* evidence is too narrow for broad conclusion;
* cases are not comparable;
* material does not match the method.

Check question:

```text
What exactly is being analyzed, and why is this material sufficient?
```

## 7. Method

The method is the procedure that transforms material into findings.

The method should not be a decorative list.

A good method section explains:

* what method is used;
* why it fits the question;
* how it is applied;
* what material it analyzes;
* what type of result it can produce.

Weak method section:

```text
The article uses comparative, semiotic, and historical-cultural methods.
```

Stronger method section:

```text
Comparative analysis is used to identify differences between traditional and contemporary architectural forms; semiotic analysis makes it possible to consider cultural elements as signs; historical-cultural analysis connects these signs to cultural memory and continuity.
```

Check question:

```text
How exactly does this method produce the findings?
```

## 8. Method and claim types

Different claims require different kinds of evidence and method.

### Descriptive claim

Example:

```text
This building uses traditional decorative motifs.
```

Required support:

* visual material;
* architectural description;
* documentation;
* photographs;
* plans.

### Interpretive claim

Example:

```text
These motifs function as signs of cultural memory.
```

Required support:

* semiotic or cultural analysis;
* theoretical framework;
* connection to source material.

### Comparative claim

Example:

```text
This project transforms traditional elements more actively than earlier examples.
```

Required support:

* comparable cases;
* criteria of comparison;
* clear analytical categories.

### Causal claim

Example:

```text
Architectural space increases active longevity.
```

Required support:

* empirical evidence;
* health or social data;
* mechanism;
* field-appropriate method.

### Correlational or associative claim

Example:

```text
Architectural parameters can be associated with conditions supporting active longevity.
```

Required support:

* conceptual framework;
* previous studies;
* clear mechanism;
* cautious but direct formulation.

### Historical claim

Example:

```text
This element derives from traditional Chinese spatial principles.
```

Required support:

* historical sources;
* architectural history;
* cultural documentation.

## 9. Architecture and active longevity warning

Claims connecting architecture and active longevity are high-risk.

They require special care.

A cultural or architectural analysis can usually support claims about:

* spatial organization;
* symbolic meaning;
* visual representation;
* cultural memory;
* environmental affordances;
* social interaction;
* everyday practices.

It cannot by itself prove medical or biological effects.

High-risk claim:

```text
Traditional Chinese architectural elements directly improve active longevity.
```

Safer analytical claim:

```text
Traditional Chinese architectural elements can be analyzed as part of an architectural environment that supports social interaction, mobility, orientation, and everyday practices associated with active longevity.
```

Rule:

If the article does not include empirical health data, avoid direct health-effect claims.

Use architectural, cultural, social, or environmental mechanisms instead.

## 10. Hidden causal claims

Many manuscripts accidentally make causal claims.

Watch for words such as:

* influences;
* determines;
* leads to;
* causes;
* ensures;
* improves;
* guarantees;
* proves;
* directly affects;
* способствует;
* определяет;
* обеспечивает;
* доказывает;
* приводит к.

These words require evidence.

If evidence is not available, revise the claim.

Example:

Weak:

```text
Архитектурная среда обеспечивает активное долголетие.
```

Stronger:

```text
Архитектурная среда формирует условия, в которых могут поддерживаться социальная активность, телесная подвижность и устойчивые повседневные практики.
```

## 11. Matching method to research question

Use this table to check alignment.

| Research question type           | Suitable methods                                         | Required material                     | Risk      |
| -------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- | --------- |
| What forms are present?          | visual analysis, descriptive analysis                    | images, plans, objects                | low       |
| How are elements transformed?    | comparative, historical-cultural, semiotic analysis      | traditional and contemporary examples | medium    |
| What do elements mean?           | semiotic, hermeneutic, cultural analysis                 | symbols, texts, visual material       | medium    |
| How do spaces support practices? | spatial analysis, observation, interviews, literature    | spatial plans, behavior data, studies | high      |
| Does architecture affect health? | empirical, sociological, medical, environmental research | data, surveys, health studies         | very high |

## 12. Common mismatch patterns

### Pattern 1. Broad question, narrow material

Problem:

The article asks about architecture in general but analyzes one object.

Fix:

Narrow the question or expand the material.

### Pattern 2. Causal conclusion, interpretive method

Problem:

The article uses semiotic analysis but concludes that architecture causes health outcomes.

Fix:

Revise the conclusion to an interpretive or environmental claim.

### Pattern 3. Method listed but not used

Problem:

The method appears only in the introduction.

Fix:

Show how each method operates in the analysis.

### Pattern 4. Object and subject too broad

Problem:

The article tries to cover culture, architecture, health, urban planning, and social policy at once.

Fix:

Define one main analytical relation.

### Pattern 5. Literature review does not create the method

Problem:

Sources are listed but do not explain why this method is needed.

Fix:

Rebuild the literature review around the research gap.

## 13. Alignment repair strategies

When alignment fails, use one of these strategies:

### Narrow the research question

From:

```text
How does traditional Chinese culture influence active longevity?
```

To:

```text
How are elements of traditional Chinese culture transformed in contemporary architectural environments associated with active longevity practices?
```

### Narrow the conclusion

From:

```text
Traditional cultural elements ensure active longevity.
```

To:

```text
Traditional cultural elements participate in shaping environmental conditions that may support practices associated with active longevity.
```

### Strengthen the method

Add:

* analytical procedure;
* case selection criteria;
* comparison criteria;
* source base;
* evidence categories.

### Strengthen the material

Add:

* more cases;
* better documentation;
* verified sources;
* empirical data, if making empirical claims;
* clear selection criteria.

### Rebuild the task sequence

Make tasks correspond to article sections.

## 14. Reviewer attack questions

A strict reviewer may ask:

* What exactly is the research question?
* Why is this method suitable?
* What material is analyzed?
* Why were these cases selected?
* How does the method produce the findings?
* Are object and subject consistent?
* Does the conclusion exceed the evidence?
* Are health or longevity claims supported by relevant sources?
* Are cultural interpretations supported by textual or visual evidence?
* Are architectural claims supported by actual architectural material?
* Are citations real and relevant?

## 15. Final alignment checklist

Before submission, check:

* Research question is explicit.
* Object and subject are not confused.
* Aim follows from the research question.
* Tasks correspond to sections.
* Material is clearly named.
* Material selection is justified.
* Method fits the question.
* Method is applied, not only named.
* Findings follow from analysis.
* Conclusion does not exceed method or evidence.
* High-risk claims have strong evidence.
* No causal claims appear without appropriate support.
* Active longevity claims are framed through supported mechanisms.
* The article does not hide weak logic behind defensive language.
